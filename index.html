Project Goals¶

For our final tutorial, we have decided to look into the public and private investments of US politicians. In 2012, the STOCK (Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge) act, which required all of the stock information regarding congressmen be released to the public. In more recent news, politicians have gotten a lot more attention for insider trading as more and more people have become aware that politicians are legally allowed to trade. We are planning to look at a dataset that was scraped from directly from the senate stock disclosure website (https://efdsearch.senate.gov/search/) to see if this access has allowed these politicians to have superior returns and to what degree. The dataset includes the senator and their respective trading data, however it lacks party affiliation, state, etc. We are going to take datasets including party and committee affiliation, and then determine if any of these affiliations have an effect on the sectors they invest in or their investment volume and success.

The first dataset comes from https://efdsearch.senate.gov/search/, and was scraped using the tools found here (https://github.com/neelsomani/senator-filings). It includes various investments from US senators dating back to 2012, when the STOCK act was passed. Investments included in this data are individual public stock in large companies, as well as investments in mutual funds and exchange traded funds. This dataset also includes a given range for the amount of each investment (i.e. $1,001 - $15,000). Also, this data can be organized by purchases and sales, as well as the date of each transaction. This will be interesting to see because we can use this information to identify when these senators entered and exited investments and then compare it to the stock or fund’s performance. We can also take a step back and see if there was any legislation passed at the time that would have affected the performance of the company that these senators were invested in.

Modeling Questions¶

Some ideas we've had going forward in terms of modeling is to test whether or not committee affiliation and party has any affect on the investment choices of the particular senator. This would require us to take additional stock data, we were thinking potentially from yahoo finance, to give us information on the sector of the ticker that is being invested in. Once we have the sector of the tickers, we can use committee as an independent variable to see what tickers they are most likely to invest in, and to see if it has any correlation with the sector of the company and the committee affiliation of the congressman. We would do this by training the dataset and running a regression model utilizing committee and party as independent variables.

Another model we are considering implementing is to compare the senators trades with the stock market and see if there are any relevant coefficients in determining investmnent volume and success. We would once again be using yahoo finance api to source the data for this. We can get the daily open and close prices for the individual tickers, and then compare them against the S&P500. We can compare their percentages of open and close value and ultimately see if senators have an advantage in investment strategy. We can also see if party affiliation, state, and committee affiliation play a role in their investment success as well.
